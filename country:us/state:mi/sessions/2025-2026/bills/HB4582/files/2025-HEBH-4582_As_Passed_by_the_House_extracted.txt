Section 1:
substitute for House BILL NO. 4582 A bill to provide for the liability of possessors of real property for injuries to invitees. the peoplE of the state of michigan enact: Sec. 1. This act may be cited as the "premises liability act". Sec. 2. As used in this act: (a) "Invitee" means an individual who is invited, expressly or impliedly, to enter or remain on premises for a commercial benefit to the possessor of the premises or for a purpose directly or indirectly connected with business dealings with the possessor. An invitation may be express or implied. (b) "Open and obvious" means the condition is known to the invitee or the condition would have been discovered by a reasonably careful person on casual inspection under the circumstances. (c) "Owner" means a person that holds legal or equitable title to premises. (d) "Person" means an individual or a partnership, corporation, limited liability company, association, or other legal entity. (e) "Possessor" means any of the following: (i) A person that is in occupation of the premises with intent to control it. (ii) A person that has been in occupation of the premises with intent to control it, if no other person has subsequently occupied the premises with intent to control it. (iii) A person that is entitled to immediate occupation of the premises, if no other person is a possessor as described in subparagraphs (i) and (ii). (f) "Premises" means real property. Sec. 3. Subject to sections 4 and 5, a possessor has a duty to use ordinary care to protect an invitee from risks of harm from a condition on the possessor's premises if both of the following apply: (a) The risk of harm is unreasonable. (b) The possessor knows or should know of the condition and should realize that the condition involves an unreasonable risk of harm to an invitee. For purposes of this act, a possessor should know of a condition if, in the exercise of ordinary care considering the character of the condition and whether the condition has existed for a sufficient length of time, a possessor exercising ordinary care would discover the condition. Sec. 4. (1) Subject to subsection (2) and section 5, a possessor owes no duty to protect an invitee from, or warn an invitee of, risks of harm from an open and obvious condition on the possessor's premises. (2) Subject to section 5, a possessor must take reasonable precautions to avoid any risk presented by any special features of a condition that is open and obvious that make the condition effectively unavoidable or create an unreasonably high risk of severe harm. Sec. 5. This act does not do any of the following: (a) Affect or impair any defense that may be available to the owner or possessor of premises under any other law. (b) Create a duty of care of an owner that is not also a possessor of the premises. (c) Impair comparative fault under section 2955a or 2959 of the revised judicature act of 1961, 1961 PA 236, MCL 600.2955a and 600.2959, or under other law of this state. Sec. 6. The supreme court, or any other court of this state, shall not adopt the standard of the Restatement of Torts, 3rd, or any other standard, that would impose a reasonable care standard, based on all of the circumstances, for premises liability, replacing the standard that imposes liability based on the legal status of the individual who is on the premises.
